Detailed Description

Contains rounding function for durations and time points.

Each of the rounding functions have a specific behaviour. If in doubt use DFL::Chrono::floor for time points and duration_cast for durations.

  • DFL::Chrono::floor always rounds durations in the direction of lower values. Meaning 0.25 becomes 0 and -0.25 becomes -1.
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds( 0), floor<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds( 0), floor<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 250)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(-1), floor<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(-1), floor<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-250)));

    The same happens for time points. But with time points positive and negative have another semantic as with durations. A positive time point denotes a point in time which is after the clock's epoch and a negative one points to a point in time which is before the clock's epoch. Hence 9 hours after the system clock's epoch returns 1970/jan/1 and 9 hours before the epoch returns 1969/dec/31.
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 1, YearMonthDay(floor<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours( 15)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 1, YearMonthDay(floor<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours( 9)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1969)/dec/31, YearMonthDay(floor<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours(-15)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1969)/dec/31, YearMonthDay(floor<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours(- 9)))));

  • DFL::Chrono::ceil always rounds durations in the direction of higher values. Meaning 0.25 becomes 1 and -0.25 becomes 0.
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(1), ceil<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(1), ceil<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 250)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(0), ceil<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(0), ceil<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-250)));
    The same happens for time points. Hence 9 hours after the system clock's epoch returns 1970/jan/2 and 9 hours before the epoch returns 1970/jan/1.
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 2, YearMonthDay(ceil<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours( 15)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 2, YearMonthDay(ceil<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours( 9)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 1, YearMonthDay(ceil<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours(-15)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 1, YearMonthDay(ceil<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours(- 9)))));
  • duration_cast behaves the same way as DFL::Chrono::floor for positive durations. For negative durations duration_cast returns the same result as DFL::Chrono::ceil.
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(0), duration_cast<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(0), duration_cast<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 250)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(0), duration_cast<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(0), duration_cast<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-250)));
  • DFL::Chrono::round always rounds durations in the direction of nearest value. Meaning 0.25 becomes 0 and 0.75 becomes 1.
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds( 1), round<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds( 0), round<Seconds>(Milliseconds( 250)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds(-1), round<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-750)));
    ASSERT_EQ(Seconds( 0), round<Seconds>(Milliseconds(-250)));
    The same happens for time points. Hence 9 hours after the system clock's epoch returns 1970/jan/1 and 15 hours after the epoch returns 1970/jan/2.
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 2, YearMonthDay(round<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours( 15)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 1, YearMonthDay(round<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours( 9)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1969)/dec/31, YearMonthDay(round<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours(-15)))));
    ASSERT_EQ(Year(1970)/jan/ 1, YearMonthDay(round<Days>(SysClkTp(Hours(- 9)))));
